================================================================================
1. WHAT CHANGED IN V10
================================================================================

V10 introduces one training-data composition change relative to V9.1.
All other parameters are identical: same base model (Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct),
same LoRA config (r=16, alpha=32), same 3 epochs, same learning rate (2e-4),
same prompt format, same dataset sources.

THE CHANGE: CTU-Malware reserved attack budget
-----------------------------------------------
In V9.1, attack samples were drawn from all sources in a single weighted pool.
Because IoT-23 (~80k attacks) and CTU-13 (~80k attacks) have much larger pools
than CTU-Malware (~60k attacks across 10 scenarios), random downsampling to
FINAL_ATTACK=120k consistently gave CTU-Malware only ~19% of the attack budget
(~23k samples) regardless of the per-source caps.

V10 adds a reserved allocation:

    CTU_MALWARE_ATTACK_BUDGET = 48,000   (40% of FINAL_ATTACK=120k)

Implementation: CTU-Malware samples are drawn first (min(budget, pool) = 48k),
then the remaining 72k attack slots are filled from other sources with the
existing SF-oversampling logic.  The raw CTU-Malware pool of ~60k comfortably
covers the 48k budget without duplication.

Effect on training composition:
    CTU-Malware attacks:  ~23k (V9.1)  →  ~48k (V10)   (+25k, +108%)
    Other attacks:        ~97k (V9.1)  →  ~72k (V10)   (-25k, -26%)

No other changes were made to the training pipeline for this run.

================================================================================
2. MEASUREMENT CHANGE: THREE-PROBE OOD BATTERY
================================================================================

V10 also formalises the OOD evaluation into a three-tier battery that was only
partially in place for V9.1.  This is a measurement change only — it does not
affect training.

    PRIMARY  — CTU-SME-11 Win7AD-1 [OOD-Hard]
                Windows 7 Active Directory server, lateral movement + Trickbot C2.
                89% REJ/RSTR rejected-connection attempts, 11% Trickbot C2 flows.
                V9.1 recall: 11.3%.  Used as primary OOD probe in all versions.

    MEDIUM   — CTU-SME-11 Amazon Echo [OOD-Easy / now reclassified OOD-Medium]
                Inbound internet scans against an IoT device (Amazon Echo).
                Structurally similar to IoT-23 training data (S0 scan flows).
                V9.1 recall: 31.6%.  Added in V10 as the "easy" OOD probe.

    FLOOR    — CTU-Malware Botnet-3 Kelihos [OOD-Floor]
                P2P spam botnet.  Per-flow traffic resembles normal web/email.
                V9.1 recall: ~3%.  Added in V10 to document the structural floor.

The three probes allow the thesis to characterise OOD performance across a
spectrum rather than a single adversarial example.

================================================================================
4. KEY FINDINGS
================================================================================

FINDING A — Win7AD-1 improvement: +75.8 pp from a single composition change
----------------------------------------------------------------------
V9.1: Win7AD-1 attack recall 11.3%  (169 / 1500)
V10:  Win7AD-1 attack recall 87.1% (1307 / 1500)

This is the central result.  The only change between runs was CTU-Malware's
share of the attack budget rising from ~19% to 40%.  CTU-Malware includes
SMB scanning, botnet C2, and port-sweep flows — structurally closer to
Win7AD-1 lateral movement than the IoT S0/UDP floods that dominated V9.1.

The V9.1 baseline analysis (thesis_notes_7.txt) established classical ML
benchmarks for Win7AD-1:
    Logistic Regression: 78.2% recall
    Random Forest:       64.9% recall

V10 exceeds both.  The narrative shift for the thesis is significant: what
thesis_notes_7 framed as "the gap classical ML cannot close" is now closed by
adjusting training data composition alone.

FINDING B — Kelihos: structural floor reclassification needed
----------------------------------------------------------------------
V9.1 Kelihos recall: ~3%   V10 Kelihos recall: 83.6%

This is unexpected.  Kelihos was designated the "hard structural floor" on the
premise that P2P spam traffic is per-flow indistinguishable from normal traffic.
Classical ML also scored ~0% MCC on Kelihos in the V9.1 era.  83.6% recall
contradicts this.

The most likely explanation: CTU-Malware training scenarios include botnet
families with overlapping flow characteristics (periodic low-volume TCP/UDP
to many IPs on non-standard ports), and V10's increased CTU-Malware budget
exposed the model to enough of these patterns to partially generalise to Kelihos.

IMPORTANT — Verify OOD status: check whether CTU-Malware-Capture-Botnet-3
(Kelihos) or a close structural analogue is included in the V10 training set
via loader_ctu_malware.py / preprocess_config.py.  If it is, the 83.6% result
is in-distribution, not OOD, and must be labelled accordingly in the thesis.
If it is not, the result is a genuine positive transfer finding.

FINDING C — Echo underperforms Win7AD-1 (reclassify from OOD-Easy to OOD-Medium)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
Echo attack recall: 65.7% — lower than Win7AD-1 (87.1%), which was the
designated hard probe.  Echo's attacks are "From_malicious-To_benign-Discovery"
inbound scans: completed (SF) connections to varied ports from external IPs.
These resemble legitimate probes and background internet noise.

Despite IoT-23 being in-distribution, and despite Echo being structurally
described as "similar to IoT-23 scans," the model does not transfer: IoT-23
scans are predominantly S0 (no response), while Echo Discovery attacks are SF
(completed handshake) — a key structural difference the model appears to track.

Action for thesis: relabel Echo as OOD-Medium throughout.  The progression
Win7AD-1 (87.1%) > Echo (65.7%) is counterintuitive and warrants one paragraph
of explanation in the Results section.

FINDING D — In-distribution performance remains near-perfect
----------------------------------------------------------------------
CTU-13, UWF, and CTU-Normal all score ≥ 98.3% on both recall dimensions.
The composition shift did not degrade known-source performance.  This confirms
that increasing CTU-Malware's attack budget at the expense of IoT-23/CTU-13
does not cause catastrophic forgetting of in-distribution attack patterns.

5. FINDINGS NOT CHANGED BY V10
================================================================================

HOST PASS-2 STILL INEFFECTIVE
    MCC +0.1711 is unchanged from V9.1's level.  Host-level aggregation via
    majority vote on flow predictions does not generalise.  This is a
    methodological limitation, not a training data problem.  Thesis treatment:
    mention as a negative result ("flow-level accuracy does not trivially compose
    into host-level attribution") and note it as future work.

================================================================================
6. THESIS NARRATIVE — REVISED FRAMING
================================================================================

BEFORE V10 (thesis_notes_7 framing):
    "Fine-tuned LLMs match classical ML on known families but fail sharply on
    OOD lateral movement.  The gap is the finding."

AFTER V10:
    "Training data composition is the dominant factor in OOD generalization for
    flow-level LLM classifiers.  A 40% CTU-Malware attack budget (vs. 19%) is
    sufficient to push Win7AD-1 recall from 11.3% to 87.1%, exceeding Logistic
    Regression (78.2%) and Random Forest (64.9%) on the same OOD set.  The gap
    documented in V9.1 was attributable to attack-family underrepresentation,
    not to a fundamental architectural limitation of fine-tuned language models."

The V9.1 → V10 comparison itself becomes the thesis's strongest evidence: a
single hyperparameter change (attack budget allocation) produces a +75.8 pp
improvement on a held-out OOD set.  This is a stronger, more principled
contribution than "our model reaches X% on benchmark Y."
