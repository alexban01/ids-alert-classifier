PROMPT FORMAT — V8 UPDATE
================================================================================

v8 extends the prompt from 11 fields to 13 by adding Dest Port and Src Port.

Input: 13 Zeek-native fields — 7 base + 2 port + 4 derived:
  Proto, Duration (s), Orig Packets, Resp Packets, Orig Bytes, Resp Bytes,
  Conn State, Dest Port, Src Port, Bytes/sec, Orig Bytes/Pkt, Resp Bytes/Pkt

Example prompt:
  Analyze this network connection and classify it as ATTACK or FALSE POSITIVE.

    Proto:              tcp
    Duration (s):       0.006303
    Orig Packets:       16
    Resp Packets:       12
    Orig Bytes:         864
    Resp Bytes:         0
    Conn State:         SF
    Dest Port:          4848
    Src Port:           46305
    Bytes/sec:          137073.4
    Orig Bytes/Pkt:     54.0
    Resp Bytes/Pkt:     0.0

Rationale: dest port is the single most discriminative feature for several attack
types that are otherwise ambiguous at the flow level (e.g. GlassFish admin on
port 4848, SMB attacks on port 445). Without port data, these flows are
indistinguishable from normal SF TCP connections.

================================================================================
V8 — CHANGES AND RATIONALE
================================================================================

Base: v7.1 (MCC +0.660, real-world benchmark)
Hardware: RunPod RTX 5090 (32 GB VRAM)

The v7.1 novel test case finding (thesis_notes_1.txt) showed the model over-relies
on conn_state. v8 addresses this by adding port features, masking conn_state during
training, and rebalancing the attack pool toward SF-state attacks.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Change 1 — Add Dest Port + Src Port to prompt
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  Rationale: conn_state, proto, and service carry most discriminative signal in
  the current 11-field prompt. Port data adds a new signal orthogonal to state —
  attacks targeting non-standard ports (4848, 445, 8080) are identifiable even
  when the connection completes cleanly (SF state). This directly addresses the
  novel test case failure on SMB exploits and credential access flows.

  Implementation: _fmt_port() helper handles float representations (80.0 → "80"),
  hex port strings ("0x50" → "80"), and NA values consistently. Added to
  prompt_utils.py alongside build_prompt() signature extension.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Change 2 — conn_state masking (CONN_STATE_MASK_PROB = 0.20)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  Rationale: The novel test case analysis showed the model treats conn_state as a
  near-deterministic signal ("SF = benign", "S0 = scan"). This heuristic is
  correct most of the time but catastrophically wrong for SF-state attacks
  (exfiltration, credential access, SMB exploits). Blanking conn_state for 20% of
  training samples forces the model to develop alternative reasoning paths using
  numeric features (bytes, packets, port) when state is unavailable.

  Implementation: make_sample() in preprocess_zeek.py applies masking at sample
  construction time. The original conn_state is preserved in the returned dict
  for use by the SF oversampling logic.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Change 3 — SF-state attack oversampling (2× weight)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  Rationale: The training pool at v7.1 is dominated by S0-state attacks (IoT-23
  port scans, botnet UDP attempts). SF-state attacks — the patterns the model
  misses most — were under-represented. A 2× weight for SF/S1/OTH attacks in the
  final sampling step increases their share in the training batch without adding
  new data.

  Implementation: random.choices() with weights=[2.0]*len(sf_attacks) +
  [1.0]*len(other_attacks), k=FINAL_ATTACK. Applied after all source loading,
  before the train/eval split.

  Practical impact (at TRAINING_FACTOR=0.1):
    SF/S1/OTH attacks in pool: 2,164 / 21,600 total (10%)
    After 2× oversampling:     ~18% of final attack sample
    Source: primarily UNSW-NB15 SF attacks

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Change 4 — Expanded reason pools
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  ATTACK_REASONS: 10 → 17 entries (7 new port/byte-aware reasons added)
  BENIGN_REASONS: 10 → 15 entries (5 new port-aware reasons added)

  Rationale: Reason diversity prevents the model from memorising a small set of
  boilerplate explanations. Port-aware reasons teach the model to cite port
  numbers in its output ("traffic targets non-standard administrative port").

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Change 5 — train.py --local / --runpod CLI flag
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  Replaces the manual batch size and gradient accumulation edits needed between
  local and RunPod runs.

  Local (RTX 3070, 8 GB VRAM):
    batch=4, grad_accum=6 (effective=24), grad_checkpointing=True,
    pin_memory=False, num_workers=0
    Note: num_workers=0 required — fork() duplicates the CUDA context into
    worker processes; those handles are invalid in child processes, causing
    deadlocks or corruption. spawn would fix it but adds complexity; workers=0
    is simpler since the bottleneck is the GPU, not JSONL loading.

  RunPod (RTX 5090, 32 GB VRAM):
    batch=24, grad_accum=1 (effective=24), grad_checkpointing=False,
    pin_memory=True, num_workers=4

  Default is local (--runpod must be passed explicitly). Forgetting --runpod on
  RunPod causes slow training but not OOM; forgetting --local on a 3070 would OOM.
  Opt-in for the dangerous direction.

================================================================================
FINDING: UWF PORT EXTRACTION BUG
================================================================================

Discovered during v8.1 analysis (2026-03-30).

Both preprocess_zeek.py and benchmark_realworld.py looked for Zeek-standard port
column names (id.orig_p, orig_p, id.resp_p, resp_p) when loading UWF-ZeekData24.
The actual column names in UWF CSVs are src_port_zeek and dest_port_zeek (Spark
output format).

Result: all UWF flows (benign and attack) had "N/A" for Dest Port and Src Port
in both training prompts and benchmark prompts from v6 through v8.

Impact:
  - UWF benign flows: model never saw realistic benign port distributions
    (port 443/80/22/53 missing from all UWF benign training samples)
  - UWF attack flows (if included): model could not learn port-based signals

Fix (v8.1): added src_port_zeek / dest_port_zeek to the fallback lookup in both
loaders. row.get("id.orig_p", row.get("orig_p", row.get("src_port_zeek", "-")))

Change 4 — LoRA rank: r=16 → r=32, lora_alpha: 32 → 64
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  Rationale: More capacity for port-aware pattern learning. The model now needs
  to associate (port 4848, ssl) → ATTACK and (port 445, S0) → ATTACK while
  keeping (port 443, ssl, SF) → FALSE POSITIVE. These are similar structural
  patterns that differ in a single feature — higher rank gives the adapter more
  directions to encode this distinction.

  VRAM impact:
    Training: ~72 MB additional (weights + gradients; optimizer states are
              paged to CPU via paged_adamw_8bit, not counted)
    Inference: ~40 MB additional (weights only)
    Both are negligible on 8 GB (3070) and 32 GB (5090).

  Speed impact: ~10-15% slower per step (more LoRA matmuls). Marginal on RunPod.

  Risk: r=32 could overfit faster on small data (TRAINING_FACTOR=0.1 validation
  run). If validation shows degraded FP recall, revert to r=16.

  Note: r=32 is plausibly overkill — the patterns are relatively simple and r=16
  already achieved 100% on CTU-13/IoT-23. The benefit is speculative but low-risk.

================================================================================
HOW LoRA RANK AFFECTS TRAINING AND INFERENCE
================================================================================

--- The rank parameter ---

LoRA decomposes the weight update ΔW into two matrices: A (r × d_in) and B (d_out × r).
Rank r is the dimension of this bottleneck.

  r=1:   Only 1 "direction" of change — extremely constrained
  r=16:  16 directions — sufficient for most narrow tasks
  r=32:  32 directions — more expressive, doubles trainable parameters
  r=d:   Full rank — mathematically equivalent to full fine-tuning (not used)

--- Effect on training ---

  Trainable parameters:    scales linearly with r
    r=16 → ~18M params (7 modules × 28 layers)
    r=32 → ~36M params

  VRAM:
    LoRA weights (bf16):  r=16 ~36 MB / r=32 ~72 MB
    Gradients:            same magnitude as weights
    Optimizer states:     paged to CPU — not in VRAM
    Total VRAM delta r=16→r=32: ~72 MB (negligible vs 8 GB)

  Speed: ~10-15% slower per step due to larger A/B matmuls.

  Overfitting risk: higher r → more capacity → can overfit small datasets faster.
  For this project: 36k samples (0.1×) is small; 360k samples (1.0×) is safer.

--- Effect on inference ---

  If running with adapter attached (PeftModel.from_pretrained):
    Extra matmul per layer: A·x followed by B·(A·x). Cost scales with r but is
    a tiny fraction of the frozen base model computation.

  If adapter is merged (merge_adapter.py before GGUF conversion):
    r is irrelevant at inference — the adapter is folded into W and the merged
    model runs as a standard dense model. Zero runtime cost.

  VRAM at inference (adapter attached): r=16 ~36 MB / r=32 ~72 MB — both negligible.

--- Practical guidance ---

  For narrow, consistent tasks (binary classification, structured output):
    r=8 to r=16 is typically sufficient. The task-specific update lives in a
    very low-dimensional subspace.

  For complex tasks requiring multi-feature interactions:
    r=32 to r=64 may help. In this project, the new requirement is learning
    (port, service, state) combinations — slightly more complex than v7's
    (conn_state, proto) patterns.

  The standard heuristic: lora_alpha = 2 × r (keeps the effective scaling
  factor α/r = 2.0 constant across rank changes). v8.1 follows this: r=32,
  alpha=64.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Key finding: port data resolved Credential Access completely
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Credential Access recall: 0% (v6, v7, v8) → 100% (v8.1)

The v7 root cause analysis concluded these flows were "unlearnable from flow
features" and removed them from training. This was incorrect. The flows target
port 4848 (GlassFish Administration Console) with SSL service — a distinctive
pattern. The failure was a port extraction bug: both the training loader and
benchmark loader looked for id.orig_p/orig_p instead of the actual column names
src_port_zeek/dest_port_zeek in UWF CSVs. All UWF port fields silently produced
N/A in every version from v6 through v8. Once the bug was fixed and Credential
Access attacks were re-added to training, the model learned the pattern in one
full training run and achieved 100% recall.

This is the central methodological lesson of the v8.1 iteration: a data pipeline
bug produced misleading benchmark results that were interpreted as a fundamental
model limitation. Systematic verification of feature extraction (confirming
column names against actual data) is required at each new data source.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Implications for thesis
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

1. v8.1 demonstrates that port features are essential for certain attack classes.
   A flow-level classifier without port data cannot learn port-targeted attacks
   regardless of architecture or training volume.

2. The Credential Access story (v7: dropped as unlearnable → v8.1: 100% recall
   after bug fix) is a useful case study in the risks of drawing conclusions from
   incomplete feature representations.

3. v8.1 MCC +0.9932 on a 2100-sample multi-source real-world benchmark is a
   strong result for the thesis. For context: classical ML on CICIDS2017 achieves

4. Remaining miss: From-Botnet-V45-TCP-Attempt (0/3) — sample size too small

================================================================================
FINDING: FLOW-LEVEL CLASSIFIERS CANNOT DETECT STEALTH HTTP C2 (v8.1 OOD TEST)
================================================================================

Discovered during v8.1 out-of-distribution testing (2026-03-31).

Dataset: CTU-Malware-Capture-Botnet-78-2 (Zeus 2.1.0.1 banking trojan)
  - Not in training data (CTU-13 maps to Botnet-42 through 54 only)
  - Zeek conn.log: 188,572 connections from infected host 10.0.2.108
  - Ground truth: Stratosphere binetflow with flow=From-Botnet-* labels
  - Test: 1000 attack flows + 1000 benign flows (stratified subsample, seed=42)

Results (v8.1 on Zeus capture):
  MCC: +0.0448  (near-random)
  Attack recall:    0.4%  (4/1000 attacks detected)
  Benign precision: 100%  (0 false alarms)

  Confusion matrix:
                         ATTACK  FALSE POSITIVE
    ATTACK                  4 (0%)    996 (100%)
    FALSE POSITIVE          0 (0%)   1000 (100%)

The model classified essentially all flows as FALSE POSITIVE.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Root cause: Zeus C2 is intentionally indistinguishable at the flow level
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Zeus communicates with C2 servers via HTTP (port 80). Example flows from the
missed attacks:

  10.0.2.108:52873 → 173.194.39.183:80  tcp SF  dur=0.29  orig=544  resp=632
  10.0.2.108:61950 → 81.88.48.95:80     tcp RSTO dur=0.34  orig=274  resp=2666

These are short TCP connections to port 80 with byte counts consistent with
normal web API calls. The model's REASON output correctly identified them as
"Short TCP session to port 80 with low byte count consistent with a web API
health check." — this assessment is accurate.

Zeus is a banking trojan engineered to blend into normal web traffic. At the
Zeek conn.log feature level (protocol, duration, bytes, packets, port,
conn_state), Zeus C2 connections are structurally identical to legitimate
HTTP requests. No flow-level classifier can reliably distinguish them.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Note on ground truth quality
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The Stratosphere binetflow labels From-Botnet = "traffic from an infected host",
not "confirmed C2 communication". This means legitimate web browsing from
10.0.2.108 (the infected host) is also labeled ATTACK. The model correctly
classified much of this as benign — because the flows ARE normal web traffic,
just originating from an infected machine. The ground truth conflates "traffic
produced by a botnet-infected host" with "malicious network activity", which
overstates the model's failure rate.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Implications for thesis
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

1. Flow-level features are insufficient for detecting stealth C2 that mimics
   normal HTTP. This is a fundamental limitation of the feature set, not a
   training data or model capacity issue. Adding more HTTP-C2 training data
   would teach "short SF TCP to port 80 = ATTACK", causing massive false
   positives on legitimate web traffic.

2. The attacks the model is designed to detect are behaviorally distinctive at
   the flow level: port scanning (S0, high packet counts), DDoS (asymmetric
   byte ratios), SMB exploitation (port 445, S0/RSTO), non-standard admin
   ports (4848). These represent a large share of real-world IDS alert volume.

3. Detecting stealth HTTP C2 requires orthogonal signals: payload inspection
   (DPI), DNS behavioral analysis (query frequency, NX domains), IP reputation,
   or temporal behavioral modeling (connection frequency, periodicity). These
   are outside the scope of a flow-level Zeek conn.log classifier.

4. The Zeus test is therefore not a failure of this model — it is a confirmation
   of the known boundary between flow-level detection and payload/behavioral
   detection. A thesis claim of "the model detects network attacks" should be
   scoped to "flow-level-distinguishable attacks" to be precise.

5. The near-zero MCC on Zeus (+0.0448) contrasts sharply with the benchmark
   MCC (+0.9932), and the benchmark covers genuinely distinctive attack types
   across four independent sources. This contrast is useful thesis material:
   it demonstrates both the strength and the precise boundary of the approach.

FINDING: STRATOSPHERE BINETFLOW GROUND TRUTH IS IP-BASED, NOT BEHAVIOR-BASED (v8.1)
================================================================================

Discovered during v8.1 OOD testing on CTU-Malware-Capture-Botnet-3 (Kelihos spam
botnet). Applies equally to the Zeus (Botnet-78-2) and Conficker (Botnet-91) tests.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Kelihos (Botnet-3) test results
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Dataset: CTU-Malware-Capture-Botnet-3 (Kelihos spam botnet, Zeus-family)
  - Not in training data (CTU-13 maps to Botnet-42 through 54 only)
  - Infected host traffic: SMTP spam (port 25), high-volume DNS, TCP probes
  - Binetflow label distribution: 1,972,733 TCP-Attempt | 104,530 SPAM |
    758,181 DNS | 53,596 TCP-Established | 1,117,623 Background
  - Ground truth: 370,550 ATTACK-labeled conn.log rows / 855 benign rows
  - Test: 1000 attacks + 855 benign (all available benign, total 1855 flows)

Results (v8.1 on Kelihos capture):
  MCC: +0.1674
  Attack recall:    38%  (385/1000 attacks detected)
  Benign precision: 77%  (659/855 benign correctly classified)

  Confusion matrix:
                         ATTACK  FALSE POSITIVE
    ATTACK                 385 ( 38%)    615 ( 62%)
    FALSE POSITIVE         196 ( 23%)    659 ( 77%)

Better than Zeus (+0.0448) but still low. The 38% recall is likely explained by
the ground truth labeling issue described below.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
The ground truth labeling problem
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Stratosphere binetflow labels flows as From-Botnet-* based on SOURCE IP, not
behavior. Every packet from the infected host (10.0.2.x) is labeled From-Botnet,
including legitimate user activity on that machine:
  - Normal HTTPS browsing → labeled ATTACK (even though it's indistinguishable)
  - Normal DNS queries → labeled ATTACK (even though they look like normal DNS)
  - SMTP spam to port 25 → labeled ATTACK (correctly — this IS distinctive)
  - TCP probes to unusual ports → labeled ATTACK (correctly — this IS distinctive)

The model correctly identifies the normal-looking traffic as FALSE POSITIVE, which
counts as a "miss" in the evaluation. The 38% detection rate likely corresponds
to the actual proportion of flows that are behaviorally distinctive (SMTP spam,
unusual port probes) vs flows that are legitimate user activity on an infected host.

In other words: the model's logic may be largely correct — the evaluation metric
is measuring "does the model flag all traffic from infected hosts" rather than
"does the model detect actual malicious behavior".

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Why the in-distribution benchmark (MCC +0.9932) does not have this problem
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The real-world benchmark uses:
  - IoT-23: device-level captures (ALL traffic from a specific IoT device is
    its full behavior — no mixed user/malware activity)
  - CTU-13: same IP-based labeling as Stratosphere, but these captures are in
    the training set so the model has seen the exact label convention
  - UWF: behavioral labels (Credential Access = specific attack action)
  - CTU-Normal: confirmed benign-only captures

For IoT-23 and UWF, the ground truth reflects actual behavior. For CTU-13, the
model has been explicitly trained on the same labeling convention (all traffic
from infected host = ATTACK), so the evaluation is internally consistent.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Implications for thesis
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

1. Stratosphere binetflow is not suitable for precise flow-level evaluation of
   models that distinguish attack behavior from normal behavior. Its ground truth
   labels entire hosts, not individual flow behaviors.

2. The OOD test results (Zeus: +0.0448, Kelihos: +0.1674) should not be

4. The model's claimed scope remains valid: it detects flow-level-distinguishable

   the Zeus nor Kelihos result contradicts this — both captures are dominated by
   traffic that looks normal at the flow level.
