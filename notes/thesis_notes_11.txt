IDS ALERT CLASSIFIER — THESIS DEVELOPMENT NOTES (continued from thesis_notes_10.txt)
Fine-tuning Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct for network intrusion detection

This file covers the V12 experiment: root-cause analysis of the V11
composition bias, the preprocessing fixes implemented before the V12 training
run, and the rationale for V12 being the final thesis model.
See thesis_notes_10.txt for V11 results and the classical ML baseline.

================================================================================
1. ROOT CAUSE ANALYSIS — V11 ATTACK COMPOSITION BIAS
================================================================================

V11 was trained at TRAINING_FACTOR=0.5 (unintentionally, due to the RTX 5090
OOM regression documented in thesis_notes_10.txt Section 4).  The benchmark
showed that Win7AD-1 attack recall regressed -29.3 pp relative to V9.1 and
was attributed to UWF composition dominance.  This section documents the
exact mechanism so it does not recur.

--- 1.1  Two compounding effects ---

Effect 1: smaller per-source pools at factor=0.5.
    At TRAINING_FACTOR=0.5, all per-source caps are halved:
        MAX_PER_SOURCE_CLASS: 40k  (was 80k)
        IOT23_FILE_ATTACK_CAP: 2.5k per file  (was 5k)
    But FINAL_ATTACK stays at 120k — the total attack budget is not scaled.
    When IoT-23 and CTU-13 caps are halved their pools shrink, leaving more
    of the 120k slots to be filled by UWF, which still has a large pool.

Effect 2: random.choices with replacement + 2× SF oversampling weight.
    The non-CTU-Malware attacks are drawn via random.choices(..., weights,
    k=n_o), which samples with replacement.  SF/S1/OTH flows carry 2× weight
    to improve recall on completed-connection attacks (C2, Credential Access).
    UWF attacks are exclusively Credential Access (short SF TCP to port 4848
    or 445) — nearly 100% SF-state.  Because SF samples get twice the draw
    probability, UWF's effective over-representation scales with its SF
    fraction, not just its pool size.

Combined: at factor=0.5, UWF's halved pool was still large relative to the
shrunken IoT-23/CTU-13 pools, and every UWF sample carried a 2× draw weight.
Result: UWF constituted approximately 44.7% of the 120k attack budget —
nearly half the attack training data was a single homogeneous tactic
(short SF TCP Credential Access), causing the model to over-learn that
signature and misclassify Win7AD-1's Trickbot C2 and REJ/RSTR flows.

--- 1.2  Evidence: composition warning at factor=1.0 ---

After upgrading to factor=1.0, a composition check (added in V11) still
fired for two sources:

    UWF:   31.8% of attacks (38,102 / 120,000) — exceeds 25% threshold
    IoT-23: 29.3% of attacks (35,137 / 120,000) — exceeds 25% threshold

UWF at 31.8% at factor=1.0 (where pools are full) confirms that Effect 2
persists independently of Effect 1: even with full pools, the 2× SF weight
causes UWF to exceed its pool-proportional share in the final draw.

================================================================================
2. PREPROCESSING FIXES FOR V12
================================================================================

Three fixes were implemented to address the composition bias.

--- 2.1  Loader-level per-source attack caps ---

UWF_ATTACK_CAP = 25,000 (new constant in preprocess_config.py).
    Applied in loader_uwf.py separately from MAX_PER_SOURCE_CLASS.
    Bounds the UWF attack pool before it enters the weighted draw.
    Benign samples from UWF remain at MAX_PER_SOURCE_CLASS.

IOT23_FILE_ATTACK_CAP: 5,000 → 3,000 per file.
    IoT-23 has ~10 active files; at 5k/file the aggregate pool exceeded 30k.
    At 3k/file the aggregate stays below 30k, keeping IoT-23 under the
    25% composition threshold after the weighted draw.

--- 2.2  Post-draw per-source hard cap with refill ---

The loader-level caps reduce pool size but cannot fully prevent Effect 2:
a source with a high SF fraction still gets over-selected in the weighted
draw regardless of pool size.  A post-draw cap was added in preprocess_zeek.py
immediately after the random.choices call:

    _src_draw_cap = int(FINAL_ATTACK * 0.25)   # 30,000 at full scale
    if any source in other_selected exceeds _src_draw_cap:
        shuffle and trim other_selected to cap per source
        refill shortfall from pool of under-represented sources,
        preserving the SF/non-SF 2× weight for the refill draw

This is a hard guarantee: regardless of factor, SF fraction, or pool sizes,
no non-CTU-Malware source can contribute more than 25% of FINAL_ATTACK to
the attack training pool.  The mechanism is transparent in the preprocessing
output — the composition warning prints for each source that required trimming.

--- 2.3  CTU-Malware attack budget: 20k → 24k ---

At factor=1.0 with 15 scenarios, the actual CTU-Malware attack pool is
approximately 23,918 flows.  The budget was 20,000, leaving ~4k botnet
flows unused.  Raising to 24,000 ensures the full pool is always consumed,
giving the model maximum exposure to diverse botnet families.

Note: the budget is not scaled by TRAINING_FACTOR (same as FINAL_ATTACK),
so on small local validation runs min(budget, pool_size) naturally falls back
to the pool size with no wasted budget.

3. REASON POOL COMPLETENESS AUDIT (V12)
================================================================================

The ATTACK_REASONS and BENIGN_REASONS pools were audited against every
training source included in V12.  Two attack families added in V11 (Murlo,
Rbot-v2) had no matching attack reasons.  The V11 hard-benign UDP broadcast
rule introduced a new benign flow type with no corresponding reason.  Four
benign port asymmetries were also identified.

--- 3.1  Missing attack reasons (added for V12) ---

Port 135 DCE/RPC exploitation — Murlo (Botnet-49):
    Murlo's flow signature is 77% TCP to port 135 with S_ state (Argus
    SYN-only, equivalent to Zeek S0).  Port 135 is Windows DCE/RPC / RPC
    Endpoint Mapper.  No existing reason described exploitation of this
    vector.  Added: S0/RSTO TCP to port 135 from outside the local Windows
    domain segment as RPC exploitation or lateral movement reconnaissance.

ICMP host-discovery sweep — Rbot-v2 (Botnet-51):
    Rbot-v2 is 99.6% ICMP with binetflow states UNK/ECO/ROB (ICMP-specific).
    The existing ICMP reason targets DoS floods ("very high packet count +
    large orig_bytes to a single destination").  Rbot-v2 sends low-rate ICMP
    echo requests to many unique destination IPs — a host discovery sweep,
    not a flood.  Added a reason distinguishing multi-host sweep (many unique
    dest IPs, small consistent orig_bytes, near-zero resp_bytes) from
    single-destination saturation.

--- 3.2  Missing benign reasons (added for V12) ---

Four benign reasons were added to balance attack-side coverage of Windows
ports that also appear in legitimate enterprise traffic:

    Legitimate SMB/CIFS (port 445):
        Three attack reasons cover port 445 exploitation (EternalBlue, worm
        propagation, Defense Evasion).  No benign reason existed for
        authorized Windows file sharing or domain authentication on port 445.

    Legitimate RDP (port 3389):
        Attack reason covers RDP brute force.  No benign complement for an
        authorized IT admin or remote worker session existed.

    Legitimate Windows DCE/RPC (port 135):
        New attack reason for Murlo requires a benign counterpart for normal
        Active Directory service discovery and COM object binding on port 135.

    Windows LLMNR/mDNS/NetBIOS UDP S0 (port ≥49152):
        The V11 hard-benign scoring rule assigns +3 points to UDP S0 flows
        matching the LLMNR/mDNS/NetBIOS broadcast pattern (700–1500 orig_bytes,
        zero resp_bytes, ephemeral destination port).  These flows appear
        frequently in the hard-benign training pool, but no reason described
        why they are benign — the model fell back to generic UDP descriptions.
        Added an explicit reason citing Windows link-local multicast name
        resolution (LLMNR, mDNS, NetBIOS) as the expected source.

Pool size after V12 additions: 77 attack reasons (was 75), 48 benign reasons
(was 44).

================================================================================
4. V12 DESIGN SUMMARY AND THESIS RATIONALE
================================================================================

V12 is not an architectural change — it is the factor=1.0 training run that
V11 was intended to be.  The V11 design (r=32, lora_alpha=64, 15 CTU-Malware
scenarios, UWF Credential Access + Defense Evasion, hard-benign UDP broadcast
rule, max_length=1024) is correct.  The V11 training run was compromised by
the unintentional factor=0.5 setting.

V12 changes relative to V11 (design, not training run):
    - TRAINING_FACTOR: 0.5 → 1.0
    - UWF_ATTACK_CAP = 25,000 (new)
    - IOT23_FILE_ATTACK_CAP: 5k → 3k per file
    - Post-draw per-source hard cap (25% of FINAL_ATTACK = 30k) with refill
    - CTU_MALWARE_ATTACK_BUDGET: 20k → 24k (full pool)
    - Reason pool: +2 attack, +4 benign (completeness)
    - Output dirs: v12-ids-model / v12-ids-lora-adapter

Expected V12 benchmark results:
    - Win7AD-1 attack recall: recovery to ~84–87% (V10 level; the V11
      regression was entirely attributable to the factor=0.5 UWF bias)
    - Echo attack recall: ≥75% (maintained or improved)
    - Kelihos attack recall: ~81–83% (stable; not the target of any V12 change)
    - IoT-23 FP recall: ~99% (preserved from V11)
    - Overall MCC: target +0.88–0.90

V12 is designated the final thesis model.  Further training iterations would
require either a new data source with qualitatively different coverage (e.g.,
encrypted C2 via legitimate HTTPS traffic) or an architectural change (e.g.,
a larger base model).  Neither is warranted given the documented OOD ceiling
and the thesis timeline.

--- 4.1  Why no further iterations are warranted ---

The primary OOD gap — Kelihos recall capped at ~81–83% — is not a training
data problem.  As established in thesis_notes_10.txt Section 7, the model
detects Kelihos via positive transfer from IoT-23 S0/UDP scan patterns, not
from family-specific training.  Adding more Kelihos-like training data is
not possible (Botnet-3 is the OOD hold-out); adding more IoT-23 data would
not change the structural generalization already achieved.

The Win7AD-1 / Trickbot C2 gap is expected to close at factor=1.0 based on
V10's 87.1% Win7AD-1 recall (which was also a factor=1.0 run).  If V12
does not recover to V10 levels on Win7AD-1 after the composition fixes, the
residual gap would warrant investigation — but should not trigger another
full training iteration without a clear hypothesis about the cause.
