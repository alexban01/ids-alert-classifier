================================================================================
1. LoRA SOUP — AVERAGING THE TWO EPOCH CHECKPOINTS BEATS BOTH
================================================================================

--- 1.1  Motivation ---

A recurring V11 finding (thesis_notes_12): epoch-1 beats epoch-2 on the
benchmark while epoch-2 wins on eval_loss — i.e. best-checkpoint selection by
eval_loss mis-selects, and epoch 2 "overfits" in the OOD sense. The
model-souping literature (Wortsman et al.'s model soups; subsequent LoRA-soup
work) predicts that *averaging* checkpoint weights generalizes better OOD than
either endpoint, turning the ep1-vs-ep2 dilemma into a free improvement instead
of a choice. LoRA adapters from the same run share initialization and
architecture, so element-wise averaging of the adapter tensors is well-defined
(392 tensors, all shapes identical).

--- 1.2  Method ---

soup = w · ep1 + (1 − w) · ep2, element-wise over adapter_model.safetensors
(checkpoint-11313 = ep1, checkpoint-22626 = ep2). No training, no GPU needed
for construction; ~seconds of CPU. Each soup saved as a normal PEFT adapter
dir with a soup.json provenance file ({soup_of, weights, method, date}):
models/v11-soup-adapter (w=0.5), models/v11-soup-w{40,60,75}-adapter.
Benchmarked with benchmark_realworld.py on the cached sample set (no --regen).

--- 1.3  Results: full interpolation curve ---

  w (ep1 weight)   MCC        Atk R    FP R     Win7AD-1 atk   Echo atk
  ------------------------------------------------------------------
  1.00 (= ep1)     +0.777     81.1%    95.8%    83.0%          77.7%*
  0.75             +0.7835      —        —        —              —
  0.60             +0.7849      —        —        —              —
  0.50             +0.7954    82.8%    96.1%    83.7%          77.7%
  0.40             +0.8008    83.4%    96.0%    83.7%          81.0%
  0.00 (= ep2)     +0.770     80.1%    96.0%    74.3%           —

  (* ep1's per-source numbers from the 2026-07-02 re-baseline. Dashes = not
  re-extracted from the report for this note; see
  results/benchmark_realworld_report.txt.)

Findings:
  1. EVERY soup beats BOTH parents on MCC — the interpolation curve is a
     clean single bump, exactly the shape the souping literature predicts.
  2. The peak (~w=0.40, MCC +0.8008) is on the EP2 side of the midpoint: the
     checkpoint that looks "overfit" on the benchmark contributes MORE than
     half the optimal mix. ep2 evidently learned real signal that benchmark
     selection alone would have discarded.
  3. The w=0.40 soup inherits the better parent per probe rather than
     splitting differences: Win7AD-1 83.7% (≥ ep1's 83.0%, vs ep2's 74.3%),
     Echo 81.0% (best of any V11 variant), benign recall 96.0% (ep2 level),
     Kelihos 44.3% (unchanged structural floor). 0 format failures.
  4. Cost: zero training. This is the cheapest MCC improvement in the whole
     project history (+2.3 pp over the previous best at w=0.40; +1.8 pp
     untuned at w=0.5).

--- 1.4  Methodology caveat (IMPORTANT for the thesis writeup) ---

Selecting w by benchmark MCC is mild test-set tuning: the benchmark stops
being a held-out measurement for the w that was chosen with it. Honest
reporting options (pick one):
  a. Report the UNTUNED w=0.5 soup (+0.7954) as the primary result and
     present the w-sweep as an analysis/ablation figure (recommended); or
  b. Select w on the eval split (never the benchmark) and then report the
     benchmark number of that single pre-registered w.
The parents (w=0 / w=1) were not selected on the benchmark, so "soup beats
both parents at w=0.5" is a clean, defensible headline claim either way.
For the same reason the left side of the curve (w<0.4) was deliberately NOT
probed further.

--- 1.5  Practical implication for all future runs ---

Soup the two epoch checkpoints of every 2-epoch run (w=0.5) before declaring
a winner — it is free and, on this evidence, dominates picking either
checkpoint. Applies immediately to v13.2 (2-epoch, just finished/finishing)
and every run after it. Also composes with everything else: souping is
post-hoc and orthogonal to data, loss, and architecture changes.

2. CONTEXT: LITERATURE REVIEW THAT MOTIVATED THIS (2026-07-15)
================================================================================

Full review in notes/research_quality_speed_2026-07.md (with sources). Items
most relevant to the thesis narrative:

  - Current LoRA config is independently validated by "LoRA Without Regret"
    (Thinking Machines, 2025; now an official TRL guide): all-linear target
    modules incl. MLP, LR ≈ 10× full-FT (2e-4), moderate batch, r=16
    capacity comfortably above this task's information content. Citable as
    external justification of the hyperparameter choices.
  - Prompt-loss-weight literature (arXiv:2401.13586; NeurIPS 2024
    arXiv:2405.14394) says for long-prompt/short-completion data (exactly
    this task) BOTH full-sequence loss (current) and pure completion-only
    loss (v14a plan) are suboptimal; optimum ≈ PLW 0.1 → v14a upgraded to a
    3-arm ablation.
  - Packing A/B confound worth one thesis sentence: packing changes
    effective samples/step (~1.46×), so v13.1-no-pack's win over v12.2 could
    be partly batch dynamics, not only cross-sample attention leak
    (arXiv:2602.09492 documents this class of confound); v13.3 (FA2+packing)
    disambiguates.
