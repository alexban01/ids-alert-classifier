V9.0 — RESULTS SUMMARY
================================================================================

v9.0 was trained on the same four existing sources (IoT-23, CTU-13, UWF-ZeekData24,
CTU-Normal) plus the new CTU-Malware-Capture series (7 scenarios: Ramnit, Ngrbot,
Htbot, Siemens, Zeus, Pushdo, Ballpit), with extended prompts including [HTTP],
[DNS], and [SSL] context sections, and max_length extended from 512 to 1024.

Benchmarked at ep1 (checkpoint-1420 out of 3558 total steps):

  Source                  Atk Recall   FP Recall   Acc
  ----------------------  ----------   ---------   ----
  IoT-23                    100.0%       98.7%      99.3%
  CTU-13                     99.7%       99.3%      99.5%
  UWF-ZeekData24            100.0%       98.7%      99.3%
  CTU-Normal                   n/a       99.7%      99.7%
  Botnet-3 OOD (Kelihos)      6.0%         n/a       6.0%

  Overall MCC: +0.775  |  Accuracy: 87.8%  |  Format failures: 0%

Compared to the v8.1 OOD baseline of MCC +0.06 for Kelihos, even the ep1 checkpoint
shows a meaningfully higher flow-level MCC. The training-set sources are well above
99% recall at ep1, indicating the model has already learned the in-distribution
patterns. Whether Botnet-3 recall improves further at ep2/ep3 is the key question.

Note: the OOD target set in thesis_notes_3.txt was MCC > +0.50. At ep1 the v9.0
checkpoint is at ~6% attack recall, still well short of that target. The 6% figure
should be understood as an early checkpoint result, not a final v9.0 outcome.

================================================================================
V9.1 — WHAT CHANGED AND WHY
================================================================================

v9.1 introduces one new signal on top of the v9.0 prompt architecture: the
[BEHAVIOR] context section. Everything else (architecture, dataset sources,
hyperparameters, max_length=1024) is identical to v9.0.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
The [BEHAVIOR] section
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

[HTTP], [DNS], [SSL] capture application-layer content for individual flows.
They help when the application layer is visible. But:

  - CTU-Malware SMTP spam (Kelihos) produces no http.log entries
  - IRC C2 (Ngrbot) produces no http/ssl entries
  - UDP-based botnets (many IoT-23 scenarios) produce no app-layer logs at all
  - conn.log-only deployments (most SIEM pipelines) have no auxiliary logs

For these cases, the only remaining signal is the statistical pattern of
connections across time: how many connections per minute from this host, how
many unique destinations, how many port scan attempts, whether the traffic is
periodic (beaconing).

The [BEHAVIOR] section adds exactly these features, computed per source host
over a 60-second and 300-second sliding window using build_behavior_contexts():

  src_conn_60s             — total outbound connections in 60s
  src_unique_dst_60s       — unique destination IPs in 60s (lateral movement / scanning)
  src_unique_ports_60s     — unique destination ports in 60s (port scan indicator)
  same_resp_port_60s       — same-port repeat count (service hammering)
  same_flow_size_repeats_300s — identical-size flows in 300s (C2 beaconing signature)
  pair_periodic_score      — periodicity score for (src, dst) pair

These features are the behavioral signature that distinguishes C2 beaconing from
normal bursty traffic even when app-layer logs are absent. A host contacting 50
unique IPs in 60 seconds is scanning regardless of what the HTTP URI says. A host
sending identical 512-byte UDP packets to the same IP every 30 seconds is beaconing
regardless of whether dns.log has an entry.

The v9.1 hypothesis is that these features, combined with the per-flow context
of v9.0, give the model enough signal to generalise to unseen botnet families.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Preprocessing rewrite (supporting change, not a thesis contribution)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The preprocessing pipeline was refactored from a single large file into separate
loader modules, and three bugs were fixed before regenerating v9.1 training data:

  - Loader row-cap bug: the CTU-Malware loader (and three others) only broke out
    of the row-buffering loop when BOTH attack and benign counts simultaneously
    reached their caps. For attack-heavy scenarios (Ramnit, Pushdo) with very
    few Normal flows, the loop read the entire conn.log into memory — potentially
    millions of rows — causing the observed freeze. Fixed to break unconditionally
    at row_cap.

  - Download timeout bug: urlretrieve() had no transfer timeout. A stalled
    Stratosphere Lab connection could hang indefinitely. Fixed with streaming
    urlopen(timeout=60) and chunk reads.

  - Sequential loaders: all loaders ran in one process. Fixed with
    ProcessPoolExecutor — relevant for iteration speed, not for training quality.

The key point for the thesis is that these bugs meant v9.0 training data for
CTU-Malware was either incomplete (scenarios that froze were skipped or partially
loaded) or absent. v9.1 training data has correct, complete CTU-Malware samples.

================================================================================
WHY BOTNET-3 OOD DROPPED BY 2.7% (v9.0: 6.0% → v9.1: 3.3%)
================================================================================

The 2.7 percentage point drop (18/300 → 10/300 correct) looks like a regression
but should not be interpreted as one. Several factors explain it:

1. EPOCH 1 NOISE — MOST IMPORTANT FACTOR

At ep1, neither model has converged. The model is still in the steep part of the
learning curve, and small differences in training data composition or batch ordering
cause meaningful variance in early checkpoint quality. A difference of 8 correctly
classified Kelihos flows out of 300 (18 vs 10) is well within the noise expected
between two ep1 checkpoints of the same architecture.

The correct comparison is ep3 vs ep3, not ep1 vs ep1. The ep1 results should be
read as a sanity check (both models are learning, neither has collapsed) rather
than as evidence of a direction.

2. THE LOADER FIX CHANGED TRAINING DATA COMPOSITION

v9.0 was trained with the CTU-Malware loader bug present. For attack-heavy scenarios
(Ramnit, Pushdo) the loader may have read only partial conn.log data before
hitting memory pressure, or the scenario was skipped entirely if it froze. This
means v9.0 training data contained fewer CTU-Malware samples than intended.

v9.1 training data has complete CTU-Malware coverage for all 7 scenarios. The model
is now seeing more botnet flow examples during training, which shifts the training
distribution slightly. An ep1 checkpoint trained on a different distribution will
naturally produce different early-checkpoint behaviour on OOD data.

This is not v9.1 performing worse — it is v9.1 training on the data it was supposed
to train on, with the side effect that the ep1 checkpoint weights are different.

3. [BEHAVIOR] CONTEXT CHANGES THE PROMPT DISTRIBUTION

v9.1 prompts include [BEHAVIOR] sections for ~60-70% of training samples (those
with sufficient temporal context for window features). The model at ep1 is
simultaneously learning the flow-level features (already well-learned in v9.0) and
the new behavioral features, which means at ep1 it may be in a transient state
where neither representation is as well-calibrated as in v9.0.

At ep1 of v9.0 the model was learning from 13-field prompts. At ep1 of v9.1 it is
learning from richer 13+behavior-field prompts. The extra prompt complexity may
cause slower initial convergence on the base features, which would show up as
slightly lower OOD recall at ep1.

4. KELIHOS DETECTION IS ALREADY NEAR-FLOOR

The key data point: v9.0 6.0% vs v9.1 3.3% are both effectively 0% for practical
purposes. The difference between "classifies 18 Kelihos flows correctly" and
"classifies 10 correctly" out of 300 is not meaningful — both models fail on Kelihos
at flow granularity. The OOD problem is not solved by either version at ep1.

The Kelihos flows that both models DO classify correctly are likely the same subset:
the flows with unusual port patterns, large byte counts, or S0/SYN states that
deviate from the expected normal traffic signature. The Kelihos flows that look like
normal TCP traffic (short SF connections) are misclassified as FALSE POSITIVE by
both models because they ARE indistinguishable from normal traffic at the flow level
without behavioral or app-layer context.

CONCLUSION ON THE DROP:

The 2.7% drop is ep1 noise on a near-floor metric with only 300 samples (8 flows).
It is not evidence that v9.1 is worse than v9.0. The meaningful comparison requires
ep3 results from both models. The thesis claim for v9.1 rests on whether [BEHAVIOR]
context improves OOD Kelihos recall at ep3 relative to v9.0 ep3, not on ep1
checkpoints.

================================================================================
WHAT TO EXPECT FROM FULL TRAINING (EP3)
================================================================================

At ep3, both models should converge on in-distribution sources to near-v8.1
levels (99%+ on IoT-23, CTU-13, UWF). The question is whether the [BEHAVIOR]
context in v9.1 translates to meaningfully better Kelihos recall.

The argument that it should:
  - Kelihos (and most botnets) exhibits distinctive behavioral patterns:
    periodic beaconing, repetitive same-sized flows, bursts to few destinations
  - These patterns ARE present in the training sources (CTU-Malware captures)
    and will be represented in [BEHAVIOR] sections during training
  - At ep3 the model should have learned to map these behavioral signatures to
    ATTACK even when the individual flow looks benign

The argument that it might not:
  - Botnet-3 is specifically held out of training, so the model has not seen
    Kelihos behavioral patterns during training
  - Behavioral features are probabilistic — a beaconing score of "high" appears
    in both legitimate NTP polling and C2 beaconing
  - The 300-sample eval is small; Kelihos behavioral patterns may not be
    well-represented in the sampled flows

The ep3 results will determine whether v9.1 moves the needle on OOD generalisation
or whether the OOD problem requires a fundamentally different approach (e.g., anomaly
detection rather than supervised classification, or host-level rather than flow-level
analysis).
