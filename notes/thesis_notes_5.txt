================================================================================
OOD PROBE SELECTION — WHY IT MATTERS
================================================================================

The OOD probe is the single metric that decides whether the v9.x work succeeded.
The in-distribution sources (IoT-23, CTU-13, UWF, CTU-Normal) are near-ceiling on
every model from v8.1 onwards — they tell us the model is working, not whether it
generalises. The OOD probe is the only honest signal.

The original OOD probe was CTU-Malware-Capture-Botnet-3 (Kelihos). As documented
in thesis_notes_4.txt, every version from v8.1 through v9.1 ep1 scored effectively
0% attack recall on it (v8.1: 38% with labelling-mismatch caveats, v9.0 ep1: 6%,
v9.1 ep1: 3.3%). The problem with Kelihos as a probe is that it communicates
predominantly over SMTP and P2P overlays on non-standard ports — traffic that is
indistinguishable at the conn.log level from background internet noise for individual
flows. The discriminating signal requires behavioral aggregation across time that a
single-flow classifier cannot access.

Because the Kelihos probe was consistently at 0%, it was impossible to tell whether
a change helped or not. The probe was measuring the ceiling of a single-flow
classifier against a fundamentally hard attack family, not the model's general
OOD generalization ability.

================================================================================
THE DARKVNC EXPERIMENT (Botnet-90 / Mixed-6) — WHAT WE LEARNED
================================================================================

Before settling on CTU-SME-11, a brief experiment was run with Mixed-6 (DarkVNC)
as the OOD source. Result on v9.1 ckpt-1186 ep1:

  Source                    Atk Recall   FP Recall   MCC
  ------------------------  ----------   ---------   ------
  Mixed-6 [OOD] (DarkVNC)      0.0%        93.9%    -0.1211

0% attack recall — worse than Kelihos. DarkVNC operates as a remote access trojan
primarily via HTTP/HTTPS on standard ports (80/443). At the conn.log level, DarkVNC
C2 traffic looks like: short SF TCP to port 443, moderate symmetric byte exchange,
clean handshake — indistinguishable from a CDN health check. This is the same
structural problem as Zeus (Botnet-78-2), which was also near-zero throughout the
v8/v9 iterations.

The lesson from DarkVNC: a useful OOD probe must come from an attack family where
the malicious signal is at least partially visible in the conn.log features the
model has access to. Purely HTTP/HTTPS C2 (Zeus, DarkVNC) and pure SMTP spam
(Kelihos) sit below the detection floor of a flow-level classifier. They are valid
real-world threats to document in the thesis, but they are not useful training-signal
probes.

The DarkVNC experiment was reverted after one commit; it was never committed as a
final result.

================================================================================
OOD REPLACEMENT — CTU-SME-11 (Amazon Echo)
================================================================================

The new permanent OOD probe is CTU-Malware-Capture-SME-11, specifically the
labeled Zeek conn.log from the Amazon Echo scenario. The attack label in the
binetflow is "From_malicious-To_benign-Discovery" — this is an infected Amazon
Echo IoT device performing network discovery/reconnaissance against the enterprise
LAN it was deployed on.

First result on v9.1 ckpt-1186 (ep1), 2026-04-04:

  Source                                Atk Recall   FP Recall   Acc    MCC
  ------------------------------------  ----------   ---------   -----  ------
  CTU-SME-11 [OOD] (Amazon Echo)          33.7%        92.9%    63.3%  +0.3297

  Confusion Matrix:
    ATTACK     : 1010 / 3000 correct (34%)
    FALSE POS  :  213 / 3000 mislabeled as ATTACK (7%)

  Host Pass-2:
    Hosts       : 1373
    Atk Recall  : 19.7%
    FP Recall   : 80.0%
    MCC         : -0.0005

Compared to the Kelihos baseline (v9.1 ep1: 3.3%) and DarkVNC (0%), a 33.7% attack
recall is a substantially more informative reading. The MCC of +0.3297 at ep1 is
well above the v8.1 OOD floor of +0.06, and above the V10.0 design target threshold
of "non-trivially-low recall".

WHY CTU-SME-11 IS MORE DETECTABLE THAN KELIHOS OR DARKVNC

The Amazon Echo scenario involves a compromised IoT device conducting horizontal
discovery across the enterprise subnet. At the conn.log level, this manifests as:

  - S0 TCP probes to many unique destination IPs (port scan signature)
  - S0 flows with zero resp_bytes and near-zero duration (no service response)
  - High orig_packets to resp_packets ratio (SYN with no SYN-ACK)

These are the exact features the model was trained to recognize from IoT-23
(horizontal port scans, S0 states with zero resp_bytes). The model can detect
them because they are structurally similar to attacks in its training distribution
— specifically IoT-23 PartOfAHorizontalPortScan and Ngrbot scanning phases.

This is both the strength of CTU-SME-11 as a probe (it gives a non-trivial signal)
and its limitation (it tests generalisation within a recognizable attack template
rather than to a wholly novel family). The thesis should document both.

WHAT THE 66% MISS RATE TELLS US

Two thirds of the Amazon Echo attack flows are missed. Looking at the label —
"From_malicious-To_benign-Discovery" — the SME-11 capture includes the full
network traffic from the infected device, not just the scanning phase. A significant
fraction of the labeled "ATTACK" flows are normal-looking TCP connections from the
Echo device to its cloud backend (Amazon services, NTP, DNS) that Stratosphere Lab
labels as malicious simply because the device is infected. This is the same
labeling mismatch documented for Kelihos in thesis_notes_3.txt: all traffic from
a compromised host gets the "malicious" label regardless of whether the individual
flow is distinguishably malicious.

The true detection rate for the actively malicious behavior (S0 scan probes) within
CTU-SME-11 is likely higher than 34%. The 66% miss rate partially reflects normal
IoT cloud-service traffic being labeled as "ATTACK" in the binetflow ground truth.

HOST PASS-2 AT EP1 IS NEAR-RANDOM

The Host Pass-2 result (MCC -0.0005 on 1373 unique hosts) indicates that the
second-pass host-level aggregation is not adding signal at ep1. This is expected:
host-level analysis is only meaningful when per-flow predictions are reliable enough
to aggregate. At ep1, flow-level recall is 34% — aggregating 34%-recall predictions
by host does not improve the signal. The Host Pass-2 metric should be evaluated at
ep3 results for a fair assessment.

================================================================================
BENCHMARK REFACTORING — BENCH_LOADERS.PY
================================================================================

The benchmark infrastructure was refactored before the CTU-SME-11 run (commit
fa5638a). The main change was extracting all data loading functions from
benchmark_realworld.py into a new module bench_loaders.py. This is a code quality
change, not a thesis contribution, but it has one practical consequence for the
thesis experiment record:

Prior to this refactor, benchmark_realworld.py contained ~800 lines mixing
inference logic, data loading, and reporting. The loader functions are now in
bench_loaders.py (~540 lines), and benchmark_realworld.py is focused on inference
orchestration. The split makes it easier to add new OOD sources (add a loader to
bench_loaders.py, reference it in benchmark_realworld.py's OOD_SOURCES list).

CTU-MALWARE SCENARIO EXPANSION — V9.1 vs V10.0
================================================================================

The original v9.0 training set had 7 CTU-Malware scenarios:
  Botnet-42 (Ramnit), Botnet-44 (Ngrbot), Botnet-52 (Htbot), Botnet-54 (Siemens),
  Botnet-78-2 (Zeus), Botnet-90 (Pushdo), Botnet-91 (Ballpit)

After the V10.0 planning notes (notes/V10.0_plan.txt), the scenario list was
expanded to 10 in preprocess_config.py (commit fa5638a):
  Botnet-42 (Ramnit), Botnet-43 (Neris), Botnet-44 (Ngrbot), Botnet-45 (Rbot),
  Botnet-46 (Virut), Botnet-48 (Sogou), Botnet-52 (Htbot), Botnet-53 (NSIS.ay),
  Botnet-54 (Siemens), Botnet-78-2 (Zeus)

Three scenarios were swapped out: Botnet-90 (Pushdo), Botnet-91 (Ballpit) removed;
Botnet-43 (Neris), Botnet-45 (Rbot), Botnet-46 (Virut), Botnet-48 (Sogou),
Botnet-53 (NSIS.ay) added — net +3.

The motivations from V10.0_plan.txt are:
  1. More botnet family diversity reduces the risk that the model overfits to the
     specific C2 patterns of the 7 original scenarios.
  2. Neris and Rbot are well-studied IRC-based botnet families that share structural
     similarity with CTU-13 Neris captures (already in training as CTU-13 binetflow),
     providing signal reinforcement across two complementary data sources.
  3. Virut (SMB self-propagation) adds worm-spread signature training data that is
     currently underrepresented.

Note: Botnet-3 (Kelihos) remains permanently held out as an OOD probe and is not
in the training scenario list regardless of version.

================================================================================
V10.0 DESIGN — OPEN QUESTIONS
================================================================================

From notes/V10.0_plan.txt, the key open questions before starting V10.0 training:

Q1: EP3 RESULTS FROM V9.1 (CRITICAL BLOCKER)
Do NOT start V10.0 before v9.1 full-training (ep3) benchmark. The [BEHAVIOR]
context section is the defining hypothesis of v9.1 — its value (or lack thereof)
for OOD recall must be established before designing v10.0. If [BEHAVIOR] improves
CTU-SME-11 recall meaningfully at ep3, v10.0 extends it. If not, v10.0 pivots.

Q2: DATASET BALANCE — CTU-MALWARE DILUTION
At TRAINING_FACTOR=1.0, CTU-Malware ends up as ~1 in 4 attack samples after the
2:1 final downsample. The V10.0 plan recommends Option B (more scenarios, now
partially done with the 10-scenario list) combined with Option A (reduce non-botnet
source caps to give CTU-Malware proportionally more attack budget).

Q3: LABELLING MISMATCH IN CTU-SME-11 GROUND TRUTH
The 66% miss rate on CTU-SME-11 ep1 cannot be interpreted as pure model failure
without understanding what fraction of the 3000 labeled attacks are distinguishably
malicious at the flow level vs. normal cloud/NTP/DNS traffic from a compromised IoT
device. A flow-level audit of the CTU-SME-11 binetflow labels (spot-check 50 missed
flows) would clarify whether the true ceiling for a flow-level classifier on this
scenario is 34%, 60%, or something else. This is a thesis robustness check.

Q4: OOD PROBE STRATEGY — KEEP KELIHOS AS SECONDARY?
CTU-SME-11 is the primary OOD probe going forward. The thesis decision is whether
to keep Kelihos as a secondary reference point (historical comparability across
v8.1 → v9.x → v10.x) or remove it entirely to simplify the benchmark. Keeping it
costs ~5 minutes of extra benchmark time per run. The case for keeping it: it
anchors the "hard OOD" end of the difficulty spectrum and lets the thesis show that
some attack families are structurally undetectable at the flow level.
