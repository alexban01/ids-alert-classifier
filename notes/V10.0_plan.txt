1. OOD PROBE: REPLACE BOTNET-3 WITH CTU-SME-11
================================================================================

Current OOD: CTU-Malware-Capture-Botnet-3 (Kelihos P2P botnet, SMTP spam C2)
  - Near-zero recall at ep1 on both v9.0 (6%) and v9.1 (3.3%) — both ~0%
  - Kelihos is difficult because it looks like normal TCP short flows at the
    flow level, with no HTTP/DNS/SSL artifacts; the discriminating signal is
    behavioral (periodic SMTP to many IPs) — hard to surface in conn.log alone
  - The probe has been uninformative: every version scores ~0% on Kelihos,
    making it impossible to tell whether a change helped or not

Proposed OOD: CTU-SME-11 (Small/Medium Enterprise capture series)
  - SME captures include mixed enterprise traffic with real malware and benign
    background — closer to deployment conditions than pure botnet captures
  - Different threat family from the CTU-Malware-Botnet-X scenarios in training,
    so it tests true OOD generalization across both attack type and traffic context
  - May produce non-trivially-low recall, making it a more informative probe

Decision to make before V10.0:
  - Replace Botnet-3 entirely, or keep both (Botnet-3 as legacy, CTU-SME-11 as primary)?
  - No loader exists yet for CTU-SME-11 in benchmark_realworld.py; will need a new
    load_ctu_sme11() function analogous to load_ctu_botnet3()
  - Verify the CTU-SME-11 URL/format on Stratosphere Lab before implementing

2. DATASET BALANCE — CTU-MALWARE SIGNAL DILUTION
================================================================================

Problem: At TRAINING_FACTOR=1.0, the dataset composition is approximately:
  IoT-23:      ~80k attacks  + ~20k benign   (capped)
  CTU-13:      ~80k attacks  + ~80k benign
  UNSW-NB15:   ~80k attacks  + ~80k benign
  UWF:           0 attacks   + ~80k benign
  CTU-Normal:    0 attacks   + ~100k benign
  CTU-Malware:  ~80k attacks + ~80k benign   (7 scenarios combined, ~11k/scenario)
  ------------------------------------------------------------------
  Total:       ~320k attacks + ~440k benign  (before FINAL_ATTACK/BENIGN caps)
  Final:        120k attacks + 240k benign   (2:1 ratio, random downsample)

CTU-Malware ends up as roughly 1 in 4 attack samples after downsampling to FINAL_ATTACK.
The other three attack sources (IoT-23, CTU-13, UNSW-NB15) contribute ~3 out of 4 attack
samples, so the model allocates most of its capacity to their signatures.

The OOD failure mode is consistent with this: unseen botnet families (Kelihos) are
not recognized because the model learned IoT DDoS and CTU-13 botnet signatures well
but botnet generalisation requires seeing more diverse botnet traffic during training.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Options (not mutually exclusive):
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

A. REDUCE CAPS ON NON-BOTNET SOURCES
   Cut IoT-23, CTU-13, UNSW-NB15, UWF, CTU-Normal to a smaller fixed cap so
   CTU-Malware scenarios have proportionally more representation in the final
   attack budget.
   Example: IoT-23 attacks → 20k, CTU-13 → 20k, UNSW → 20k, CTU-Malware → 60k
   Risk: may hurt in-distribution accuracy on IoT-23/CTU-13 probe sources.

B. ADD MORE CTU-MALWARE-BOTNET SCENARIOS
   Expand beyond the current 7 scenarios. Stratosphere Lab has many additional
   CTU-Malware-Capture-Botnet-X captures (different families, years, protocols).
   More botnet diversity → better chance of learning botnet-agnostic features.
   This is the least disruptive change; keeps other sources intact.
   Action: survey available scenarios, add 5-10 more to SCENARIOS list in
   preprocess_config.py / loader_ctu_malware.py.

C. WEIGHTED SAMPLING IN FINAL DOWNSAMPLE
   Keep all sources at their current caps but in the final 120k attack budget,
   allocate a fixed fraction to CTU-Malware (e.g., 60k of 120k = 50% botnet).
   This oversamples CTU-Malware relative to its raw count without changing caps.
   Requires a change to the final downsample logic in preprocess_zeek.py.

D. SPLIT INTO TWO SPECIALIST MODELS
   IoT model:  IoT-23 + CTU-Normal (Zeek conn.log native, IoT deployment)
   CTU model:  CTU-13 + CTU-Malware (mixed enterprise / botnet deployment)
   Pros: each model trains on a coherent domain without cross-domain noise.
   Cons: two models to train, maintain, and deploy; ensemble or routing logic needed.
   This is the highest-effort option; consider only if single-model approaches fail.

E. DROP UNSW-NB15
   UNSW-NB15 adds 80k attack + 80k benign samples but is the most synthetic
   source (older Bro-generated features, not live Zeek). It may be adding noise
   rather than signal for botnet detection. Try removing it and reallocating
   the attack budget to CTU-Malware.

Recommended starting point for V10.0: option B (add more CTU-Malware scenarios)
combined with option A (reduce non-botnet attack caps) — easiest to implement,
most direct address of the dilution problem.

Note on TRAINING_FACTOR: cannot raise it to 1.0 for full runs on local hardware.
The row_cap memory issue was fixed in v9.1 for CTU-Malware (TASK-008), but full
factor on an 8-core local machine still exceeds RAM for multi-scenario parallel
loading. Use TRAINING_FACTOR=0.1 for local validation; TRAINING_FACTOR=1.0 on
RunPod for the real run.

4. OPEN QUESTIONS BEFORE V10.0 TRAINING
================================================================================

Q1: Which OOD probe gives a more informative signal — CTU-SME-11 alone, or
    CTU-SME-11 + Botnet-3 in parallel? Keeping Botnet-3 preserves historical
    comparability; replacing it makes the benchmark cleaner.

Q2: What is the right attack:benign ratio for a CTU-Malware-heavy dataset?
    If CTU-Malware scenarios are attack-heavy (e.g., Ramnit has ~200k botnet
    flows and ~500 normal), the row_cap fix means we now load full scenarios —
    but the benign budget may be CTU-Malware-benign-limited. May need to
    re-examine per-scenario benign counts before setting caps.

Q3: At ep3 of v9.1 full training, does [BEHAVIOR] context improve OOD recall?
    This is the outstanding question from v9.1. The ep3 results determine whether
    v10.0 should keep [BEHAVIOR], extend it, or pivot to a different approach.
    Do NOT start V10.0 before v9.1 ep3 benchmark is complete.
